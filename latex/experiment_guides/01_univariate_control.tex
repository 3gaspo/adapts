\section{Theoretical overview and goal}
A forecasting adapter is useful only if it improves a well-defined, untouched
backbone.  For a look-back $x\in\mathbb{R}^{L}$, a pretrained forecaster produces
$\hat y_0=f(x)\in\mathbb{R}^{H}$.  The univariate control evaluates $f$ directly,
without retrieved neighbors, gates, or trainable adaptation.  It therefore
separates backbone error from error introduced by the retrieval and adaptation
pipeline.

The main metrics are
\[
 \operatorname{MSE}=H^{-1}\lVert \hat y_0-y\rVert_2^2,
 \qquad
 \operatorname{nMSE}=H^{-1}\left\lVert
 \frac{\hat y_0-y}{\sigma(x)+\varepsilon}\right\rVert_2^2.
\]
Per-user aggregation and the worst 10\% user mean reveal whether a good global
mean hides systematic failures on a subset of series.

\goal{Establish the no-adaptation reference, measure its temporal and user-wise
heterogeneity, and provide the denominator against which every retrieval method
must be compared.}

\section{Implementation details}
Dates are split chronologically as $(\Tzero,\Tone,\Ttwo,\Tthree)=(0.30,0.35,0.15,0.20)$.
The control command maps \Tone, \Ttwo, and \Tthree to the labels
\code{train}, \code{oracle}, and \code{eval}; the Slurm job evaluates the held-out
\Tthree period.  Each query contains all users at one date, but the backbone is
called as a univariate model with one channel per sample.

user scatter plots, and prediction examples.  The primary comparison is the
same \Tthree MSE/nMSE later used by adapted methods.

\section{Interpretation}
This experiment is descriptive rather than trainable.  Any adapted method must
beat the control on the same queries and aggregation rule.  Improvements on
\Tone or \Ttwo alone are insufficient evidence because those periods are used
to fit adapters or gates.
